My Step‑by‑Step Process

I began by opening the transaction on Etherscan. My goal was to understand the transaction from end to end — not just what happened, but why it happened and what each field tells me about the behaviour of the network.

1

Identifying the Sender and Receiver

The From address is the wallet that initiated the transaction. The To address is the wallet receiving the ETH. This is a simple transfer between two externally owned accounts (EOAs), which means there are no smart contracts involved and no internal transactions.

2

Understanding the Value Sent

The sender transferred 0.374798792129039 ETH. This amount is separate from the gas fee — the receiver only gets the value, not the fee.

3

Breaking Down the Gas Fee

Gas Used: 21,000 (standard for ETH transfers)
Gas Price: 3.213708141 Gwei
Fee Paid: 0.000067487870961 ETH (~$0.13)

Advanced Details ▸
  • Low gas price indicates low network congestion
  • 21,000 gas is the minimum required for a transfer
  • Fee confirms efficient inclusion in the next block
  • Gas fees breakdown: Base Fee 0.960290033 Gwei, Max Fee 3.213708141 Gwei, Max Priority Fee 3.213708141 Gwei.
  • Burnt fee: 0.000020166090693 ETH ($0.04).
4

Checking Block Inclusion

The transaction was included in block 24355925 and confirmed within 30 seconds. This tells me the gas price chosen was appropriate for the network conditions at the time.

5

Reviewing Logs and Internal Transactions

Because this is a simple ETH transfer, there are no logs or internal calls. This contrasts with DeFi transactions, where logs and internal calls reveal most of the interesting behaviour.

6

Summarising the Transaction

In plain English: one wallet sent 0.3748 ETH to another wallet and paid a small gas fee of 0.000067 ETH. The transaction was processed quickly, required no contract interactions, and behaved exactly as expected for a standard ETH transfer.
